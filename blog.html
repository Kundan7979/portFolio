Jaitu Block, Faridkot District, Punjab

Chapter-1

Cluster Profile

1.1 Regional Setting of the Cluster-Jaitu also known as GangsarJaitu is a Municipal Council in Faridkot district. It’s 30 km from Bathinda and 34 km from Faridkot. Faridkot according to a legend was founded by Raja Mokalsar who built a fort named Mokal Har some 700 years ago. Some describe the founding of the place even earlier when Bhatties came to occupy this place from the South. The name of the place was changed to its present name after the name of BABA FARID JI, a Muslim Sufi saint of repute.Jaitu was a small village founded by Baba Jaitu and took its name "Jaitu" from him.Faridkot is mainly an agricultural district as 70% of the population resided in the rural areas. Further agriculture alone provides the single largest source of employment and livelihood to 68% of its main workers i.e. agriculture labourers. The two main crop seasons in a year in the district are kharif and rabi which is locally known as ‘Sauni’ and ‘Harrhi’. During Kharif more than 70% area comes under paddy while 15% has been occupied by cotton crop. During Rabi more than 90% area comes under wheat crop and the rest under other minor crops. Kinnow, Guava cultivation is also are catching on fast in the district.


1.2 Location- Jaitu is in the northwestern region of India and is a part of the Indo-Gangetic alluvial plains which in a macro regional context forms a part of great Satluj Ganga plain. The exact cartographic co-ordinates of Jaitu are 30.450893°N 74.882898°E. It has and average elevation of 201 meters (660 ft). The district is situated in the dry region of the state. The climate is extremely hot and dry in the summer and severe cold in the winter.The surrounding districts are Muktsar,Bathinda,Ferozepur,Moga and Barnala in Punjab and Sirsa in Haryana. The boundaries of the states of Haryana & Rajasthan is also under an hour's drive from Jaitu. Total area of Jaitu is 405 km² including 394.92 km² rural area and 9.60 km² urban area. There are about 42 villages in Jaitu block. The official language of Jaitu is Punjabi. It is a low-lying flat area. The surface of the district is depositional plain which was formed by alleviation by the rivers in the remote past. No river is flowing through the district, but there are some drains which flow during heavy rains and serve as natural drainage. There is a vast network of canals i.e. Bikaner, Sirhind feeder and Rajasthan Canal passes through Faridkot district. Sirhind feeder, Rajasthan Canal and Abohar Branch of Sirhind canal run through the entire length of Faridkot district in north-south and northeast-southwest directions respectively.


1.3 Evolution of the cluster-Jaitu area is known for the best staple of cotton. Most of the area is covered under sandy soil followed by clayey soil except some patches where there is appreciable thickness of top clay layer varying from 6.7 to 16.7m. This area is known for cotton cultivation but with the constant inclement weather, excessive and untimely rains followed by humid conditions, the farmers have been compelled to switch over to paddy cultivation since 1995, as cotton crop has been attacked severally by American boll worms and has not providing the required returns. However to retain the previous cultivation of cotton, the farmers are being persuaded for cotton cultivation using BT seed. Faridkot district lies in Malwa belt of the Punjab and it was known as the Cotton belt of Punjab but now due to the high usage chemical fertilizers and pesticides, it has become the Cancer belt of Punjab with highest number of health issues to human and environment. Kheti Virasat Mission has been promoting the organic farming in the area for the last 15 years. With its constant and selfless efforts, a number of farmers have started doing organic farming and move towards multi-cropping. Hence, breaking the monoculture of wheat and rice. In the 30 villages of Jaitu block, 3000 women have been growing organic seasonal vegetables through organic kitchen gardens. These women are conserving the native seeds and doing the food processing of the surplus produce. These women are potential in providing the nutritional and health security at the household level. These very women are involved in TRINJAN initiative of Kheti Virasat Mission that is the “Revival of Traditional Skills through Rural Livelihoods”.


1.4 Demography and Growth Trend- According to Census 2011 information, Jaitu has a population of 1, 60,930 peoples. There are 30,981 houses in the sub-district. Rural households are 23,751 and urban households are 7230. Rural males constitute 65,027 and rural females constitute 58,526 of the total population. Urban males are 19,880 and urban females are 17,497 of the total population. Total males and females population is 84,907 and 76,023 respectively. As of the 2001 India census, Jaitu had a population of 33,465. Males constitute 53% of the population and females 47%. Jaitu has an average literacy rate of 62%, lesser than the national average of 74%: male literacy is 67%, and female literacy is 56%. In Jaitu, 13% of the population is under 6 years of age.


1.5 Socio-EconomicAspects -Jaitu's main economy is agriculture. It is known for its mandis which is the local word for market, they are famous for wheat and paddy trade. There are different processing industrial units. There are around 30 rice mills and 3 cotton factories. There are some timber saw mills also. People are not continuing anymore in the traditional occupations due to the lack of raw material’s availability and no demand as cheap products are available in the market. Traditional skills are spinning, weaving, embroidery, carpentry, blacksmithery and others. People specially the youngsters are migrating to abroad for jobs and career as they think there is no opportunity in Punjab. Agriculture has become the least preferred work and choice.


A study, carried out amongst mentally retarded children in the Malwa region of Punjab, revealed 87% of children below 12 years and 82% beyond that age having uranium levels high enough to cause diseases. Unscientific farming practices, that emerged after the introduction of Green Revolution, are also alleged to be a reason for growing incidence of not just cancer but also, high rates of spontaneous abortions, reproductive aliments, genetic deformities, anaemia, diarrhoea, vomiting, fluorosis and a host of skin ailments including rashes and boils.


A 2007 epidemiological study found that the surface waters of Malwa region are contaminated with arsenic, cadmium, chromium, selenium and mercury primarily due to the discharge of untreated waste water from surrounding industries. With increasing poisoning of the soil, the region once hailed as the home to the Green revolution, now due to excessive use of chemical fertilizer, is being termed the "Other Bhopal", and "even credit-takers of the Revolution have begun to admit they had been wrong, now that they see wastelands and lives lost to farmer suicides in this 'granary of India.


1.6 Human Development Aspects - During the post-independence period, Punjab witnessed a high level of economic prosperity. The Green and the White Revolutions ushered in economic growth as seen from high per capita incomes. High levels of economic development gave Punjab a relatively high HDI. Punjab has an HDI value of 0.537. Only Kerala has a higher value of 0.638.


However, the high levels of development did not translate into progress for women. Society remained feudal and women’s development abysmal resulting in a low gender development index.


1.7 Key Economic Activities in the Region- Agriculture and animal husbandry are the main source of livelihood in this area. The primary sector includes agriculture and related activities. Apart from this, people are involved in personal business and government jobs too. These days youngsters are migrating to abroad for getting the employment.


1.8 Infrastructure: Social, Physical, Financial and Production related- In the area, there are women who are willing to work and want some sitting work that can be done from the house itself. For this they have the basic infrastructure needed e.g. the spinning wheel, handloom, crochet, knitting needles, darri loom etc. These women need initial financial support in order to get new equipments and tools that have got old as they have been using it since long time. After seeing the elder women working, young generation is also getting interested to do the hand based work.

2.2 Production Process


Cotton Procurement and Spinning


Organic/ Desi short staple cotton is purchased from the farmers.


After ginning and carding of the raw cotton, the lint is given to the rural women for spinning on the desi/ traditional charkhas.


Sliver and poonis are made by the women in order to do spinning and make yarn on the charkhas.


Spinning Process


Natural Dyeing


Locally available raw material is used for making natural colors. It its babul bark, eucalyptus leaves, pomegranate skin, onion skin, neem leaves and marigold flowers.


Raw material for dyeing is soaked in water for a couple of days and then boiled for an hour, after this the wet yarn is put into the boiling water and kept in it for another half an hour with continuous stirring.


Weaving


After dyeing, the yarn is warped and sizing is done. Bobbins are made and fabric is woven on the traditional looms.



2.3 Value Chain Analysis


  • Field of desi/native short staple cotton is low as compare to the commercial cotton. Farmers look for a premium price as the cotton is fully organic without any chemical fertilizer and chemicaYl pesticide .It needs extra care and caution.

  • After the picking of cotton, storing the huge quantity of cotton requires proper facilities. For the organic cotton ginning and carding can’t be done in the same system where the commercial cotton is processed.

  • Women who do spinning need training for making finer and thinner yarn.

  • A lot of research and development and standardization is needed in making various colors from natural dyeing.

  • Looms are of old style that have less width. In order to increase its width for more utility the loom size needs to be increased and the weavers have to be trained accordingly.

  • New designs and garments have to be developed as per the market demand.

Chapter-3

Market Assessment and Demand Analysis


Synthetic clothing and synthetic colors have a lot of health hazards and cause environmental destruction. Chemical dyes are a big cause of polluting rivers and water bodies. Bt cotton and other synthetic fabric cause skin diseases, cancer, pigmentation, irritation and a lot more problems due to the use of chemicals in its processing. Gradually, along with organic food, the demand for organic clothing and natural dyes is also increasing. People want a socially and ecologically responsible products where a fair wage is given to one’s hard work and it’s in harmony with nature too. TRINJAN products are high in demand among the people who are associated with the organic farming movement in the country. For other common people it becomes a little expensive as a number of people are involved in the processes and all the work is done manually. Each of the artisan associated is paid with dignity. So far demand is higher than the production. A lot of awareness programs are done in rural and urban areas of Punjab, it includes cultural festivals, folklores, literary workshops, singing, folk dance, poems etc. We are doing offline marketing as of now, online marketing has to be developed for making a larger outreach of these artistic products.

In the present scenario with regard to Punjab’s agriculture and environment, Kheti Virasat Mission (KVM) began its awareness- and discourse-building work from 2005 onwards. Various aspects related to intensive agriculture paradigm were discussed in repeated dialogues with farmers and other stakeholders including doctors, consumers, civil society groups, students, business people, government officials etc. Through its tireless work, Kheti Virasat Mission has created a debate and discourse on alternative paths to agricultural development amongst farmers, consumers, academics and the government. It has also led to many farmers successfully shifting to organic farming in this heartland of the Green Revolution. In 2015, KVM had celebrated its 10 year-long journey towards achieving the “Evergreen Revolution” and this proposal is also in connection to this noteworthy development.



KVM is committed to provide people with safe and healthy food with environmental security. A lot of efforts have been made to understand and revive the traditional practices of agriculture and water conservation. Primarily, KVM started working with the farming community and tried to address the various problems faced by today's agricultural system. Gradually, KVM as an organization and a part of bigger social movement (organic farming movement) have attempted to look at other issue in the society as well.

KVM’s Interventions


KVM has pooled successful master organic farmers from across India and initiated a series of workshops in different parts of Punjab, to bring confidence in Punjabi farmers around ecological farming, and to build their capacities. To substantiate the argument of farming without pesticides and to give scientific reasoning to the strength of organic farming, KVM also roped-in known agriculture scientists to address the farmers. Opportunity was provided to the farmers to visit organic farms in different parts of India as exposure trips so that it helps in better visualization and can serve as a source of motivation to begin with the organic farming. In the last 15 years, KVM has undertaken multi-dimensional approach to engage with the concerned strata of society and build awareness through series of public dialogues, seminars, conferences, workshops and food and cultural festivals on broader issues of Sustainable Agriculture, Environmental health (particularly the health impacts of intensive agriculture), Water and Ecological Crisis, issues related to food security, seed diversity and food safety.


  • Farmers Capacity Building

As by now it’s well known that Green Revolution has done more harm than good to the soil and people of Punjab by promoting the monoculture model of chemical farming. During its 15 years of existence, KVM has organized more than 1000 of Workshops, Seminars, Organic Farming Training Camps, village level meetings and more than 500 Farmer Field Schools covering more than 24,000 farmers across all the districts of Punjab. KVM has undertaken several field studies and campaigns to build a social movement around ecology, health, toxicity, safe food and sustainable agriculture. This covers a vast arena of field visits to the problem areas and mass education. A lot of farmers are associated with KVM who have been doing organic farming successfully and keep sharing their experiences with other fellow farmers. It has also led to many farmers successfully shifting to organic farming in this heartland of the Green Revolution.   • Women Action for Ecology Initiative

While working with the male farmers, a need to work with the women farmers was realized as they are important stakeholders in the agriculture. Before Green Revolution women would work equally with the men in the fields, but these 3-4 decades of chemical intensive and market based farming did not allow women much to be part of the farming community. Women do take care of the household and when they got to know about being serving poisonous food to their children, they became so unhappy about it. A group called “Women’s Action for Ecology” was established. Under this initiative, a large number of organic kitchen gardens were started in Barnala and Faridkot districts of Punjab. Till date in both these districts there are about 6000 kitchen gardens in 60 villages. Women along with providing safe and nutritional seasonal vegetables to their family are also able to save monthly income. After fulfilling the household needs they do sell some of the vegetables in the village itself that gives them an extra source of income. These women are also multiplying seeds to disseminate among women with a theme – “Grow Safe Food and Feed your Children with Safe Food”. This activity is also contributing in making these women self-dependent in vegetables and greens. This saves their money, ensures health and brings back the tradition of self-reliant villages to some extent.

The Haat is established in the cities namely: Patiala, Jalandhar, Ludhiana, Kotkapura, Muktsar, Barnala, Chandigarh, Bhatinda and some semi regular markets in the form of Kudrat Huts in Gurdaspur, Muktsar, Jaitu etc. We are also establishing Permanent stores namely Kudrat Huts since the consumers are demanding regular supply of the organic produce. Seeds from different parts of the country like Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh etc. have also been collected and multiplied. Farmers have set up their own seed banks with different varieties. This initiative is leading a large number of farmers to conserve seeds of hope for the wellbeing of all.

  • TRINJAN: Rural Livelihoods through Traditional Skills Weaving the Social Fabric with Compassionate warp and Dedicated Weft

TRINJAN used to be a place or platform in the previous times where women of a village would get together and do the spinning, embroidery and other kinds of works. To revive this culture, a cotton project has been started where cotton is organically produced by the farmers and would be hand spun and hand woven in traditional manner. This would provide a livelihood opportunity and at the same time, it takes us back to our culture that is about to vanish if no affirmative actions are being taken. TRINJAN was very prominent social unit which stood for liberty, wisdom, self-expression and self-empowerment among women. As an economic measure, TRINJAN served as a common facility for production of handmade products. For the young of the village, it was an important institution for skill learning and value education. Kheti Virasat Mission (KVM) has made a start in Faridkot to revive this women driven tradition while linking it to current concerns of environmental degradation. The use of organic desi cotton is emphasised in the TRINJAN workshops thereby also promoting the breaking the monoculture of rice/ wheat. The TRINJAN model now aims to extend itself as a viable socio-economic alternative for Institution Building for grass-root efforts to revive the village crafts in an increasingly patriarchal, decontextualized and deskilled Punjabi society.

Project Concept and Strategy Framework


6.1 Project Rationale


TRINJAN: Building the Social Fabric with Compassionate Warp and Dedicated WeftTRINJAN is the symbol of caring and sharing in Punjabi community. Under this umbrella, women used to spin, weave, knit, sing, celebrate, appreciate and create different kinds of artistic products. It used to be a platform of collective teaching and learning with a close association of nature and culture. The type of development model which is being adopted in last 5-6 decades didn’t allow women to enhance the collective working and its spread to the next generation. Younger generation is getting school and college education where these traditional creative skills are not considered worthy. That’s why the spinning wheel which is a symbol of life is being burnt by people in chullah as fuel wood. Same has happened with the handlooms and other material used for handicrafts and textile items. With this background and zeal to prevent this great traditional art from being vanished, KVM started working for its revival. For us, these are not mere products that come out, there is a connect of human and nature that can be clearly seen along with the association and owness. In order to preserve this heritage, Organic Desi Cotton is being grown by the farmers, procured by KVM, hand spun, naturally dyed and hand woven by the rural artisans. Along with this there are artisans who do hand embroidery, crochet, Knitting, make different grass crafts and other decorative products. All these skills have been promoted and developed under TRINJAN. It has freedom for the artisans as they can work from home, most of the artisans are women. It’s a very good opportunity for the livelihood generation and pass on this skill to the next generation. KVM believes that revival of traditional, slow, natural and cultural wisdom shall help addressing the physical, mental and emotional health crisis that people have been going through. These are socially and ecologically responsible products


6.2 Project Activities


To establish a self-reliant and eco-friendly ecosystem on the method of ‘Farm - Fabric - Fashion’


Farm: To make farming community self-reliant and Eco-friendly through:

  • the dependency on BT cotton

  • Introducing Desi Short Staple organic cotton

  • Integrating organic crop cycles annually breaking the tendency towards monoculture


Fabric: Reviving the women led traditional industries of hand-spinning and hand-weaving

  • Introducing auxiliary units/ technology for processing cotton into fabric

  • Establishing a common service center as a resource and workspace for the artisans

  • Providing meaningful skill training to the women and youth and creating a streamlined production and delivery mechanism


Fashion: Creating marketable USP with innovative handmade products

  • Market oriented Design led intervention interfacing tradition with contemporary that command higher value

  • Creating a brand for the products from the cluster

  • Structure a calendar of marketing events and associations with marketing platforms to keep the sales cycles running


6.3 Focus Products/Services

Different kinds of creative products include bedsheets, khesh (topsheet), towel, stoll, muffler, fabric for garments, darri, mats, rugs, woolen sweater, hand woven basket, crochet earrings, toran, coasters, wall hangings etc. are made. Women are willing to work and try new things that are in demand and liked by people. Gradually, this work has been expanding.

Chapter-7

Project Interventions


Soft Intervention:

  • Mobilizing people for the organic desi cotton and hand made products

  • Making awareness among producers and consumers both

  • Conducting training and workshops among the artisans for different skills by experts in the respective fields


Hard Intervention:

  • Providing artisans with the upgraded tools and equipments in order to have a variety in the products

  • Establishing a center or physical space where artisans could carry out their work individually or collectively

  • Setting up the carding and ginning machineries for having pure organic cotton to be processed

  • Proper storage facility for the cotton, yarn and final products

  • Advanced spinning wheels, handlooms and sewing machines

  • Eco friendly packaging and labelling of the products.
